## Writing Style (MANDATORY — applies to ALL output fields)

Write for a busy PA coordinator who needs to make decisions fast. Follow these rules strictly:
- **Short sentences.** Maximum 20 words per sentence. Break long sentences into two.
- **Plain English.** No clinical jargon unless it is a specific lab name or metric (CRP, ESR, CDAI). Never use words like "correlational profile", "vulnerability", "compounded risk", "cumulative", "paradox", "formality", "compensate".
- **Lead with the number.** Start findings with the key statistic, not a description. Example: "86% of approved cases had CRP above 35" — NOT "The patient's severe inflammatory profile correlates with approval patterns."
- **One idea per sentence.** Never chain ideas with "but", "despite", "suggesting", or "creating". Split into separate sentences.
- **Use "approved" and "denied" not "favorable" and "at-risk".** Speak in terms of payer outcomes.
- **Name the gap plainly.** Say "TB screening" not "TB_SCREENING_PENDING". Say "hepatitis B screening" not "HEPATITIS_B_SCREENING_PENDING".

## Instructions

Synthesize the per-gap statistics AND per-gap differentiator insights into cross-gap intelligence. Your unique value is finding patterns that only emerge when looking ACROSS multiple gaps simultaneously.

### analysis_strategy
- **Exactly 1 sentence.** State what you analyzed and how. Format: "Analyzed [N] similar [medication] cases with [payer], comparing approved vs denied outcomes for [N] documentation gaps to identify clinical patterns that predict payer decisions."

### overall_risk_assessment
- **Maximum 2 short sentences.** State the single most important finding and the overall risk level (low / moderate / high). Do NOT list lab values — the patient profile is already shown elsewhere.
- Example: "22-30% of similar cases with these gaps were denied. High inflammation markers strongly correlate with approval despite missing screenings."

### hidden_insights
- Up to 3 patterns that ONLY emerge when looking across multiple gaps together.
- **headline**: Maximum 8 words. Plain statement of the pattern. Must be immediately understandable without context. Example: "High CRP drives approval across all gaps" — NOT "Severe inflammation signal overcomes screening gaps consistently."
- **finding**: Exactly 1-2 short sentences. Lead with the key number. Each sentence must stand on its own — a reader should understand it without reading anything else on the page.
- **Clarity rules for findings:**
  - State WHICH sub-cohort you are describing: "Among cases missing TB screening, 86% of approved cases had fecal calprotectin on file."
  - NEVER say "[metric] across [gap] gaps" — this is ambiguous. Instead say "For each of the 3 screening gaps, [metric] appeared in X% of approved cases."
  - When referencing a lab test or document as a differentiator, explain WHY it matters: "Fecal calprotectin on file — a marker of GI disease severity — appeared in 86% of approved cases missing TB screening."
  - When stating the patient's position, be direct: "This patient does not have fecal calprotectin on file."
- Each insight MUST reference specific data from the per-gap insights or patient profile.
- If per-gap analyses reported "weak" evidence strength due to small cohorts, note this limitation explicitly.

#### Headline rules:
- The headline must name the SPECIFIC thing (a lab, a document, a treatment count) and a KEY NUMBER.
- Format: "[Thing]: [number]" — e.g., "Fecal calprotectin on file: 86% approved", "3+ prior treatments: 100% approved"
- NEVER use vague verbs like "creates", "drives", "correlates with", "overcomes" in headlines.
- NEVER reference gap names in headlines. The gaps_involved field already shows which gaps are relevant.

#### Finding rules:
- Start with the data: "For both hepatitis gaps, 86% of approved cases had fecal calprotectin on file vs 0% of denied."
- Then state the patient's position: "This patient has fecal calprotectin documented (1450 ug/g)."
- NEVER say "[thing] missing creates [gap] risk" — say "X% of approved cases had [thing] vs Y% of denied."
- Use plain gap names: "TB screening" not "TB_SCREENING_PENDING", "hepatitis B screening" not "HEPATITIS_B_SCREENING_PENDING".

#### ANTI-PATTERNS — Do NOT produce insights like these:

BAD headline: "Prior treatments missing creates TB gap risk"
WHY BAD: "creates TB gap risk" is vague. What does the reader do with this?
GOOD headline: "3+ prior treatments: 100% approved for TB gap"

BAD headline: "Formal severity scores correlate with denial"
WHY BAD: Sounds like severity scores CAUSE denial. Confusing.
GOOD headline: "Formal severity scores: 100% of denied cases had them"

BAD headline: "Missing fecal calprotectin creates consistent risk"
WHY BAD: "creates consistent risk" is vague. Reader cannot tell what action to take.
GOOD headline: "Fecal calprotectin on file: 86% of approved cases"

BAD finding: "Cases missing TB screening averaged 3.14 prior treatments when approved vs 2.0 when denied."
WHY BAD: "averaged 3.14 prior treatments when approved" is hard to parse. What is 3.14 prior treatments?
GOOD finding: "Among cases missing TB screening, approved cases had 3+ prior treatments vs 2 for denied cases. This patient has 3 documented prior treatments."

### patient_position_summary

- Example: "Patient's inflammation markers place them in the approved cohort for most gaps. Missing fecal calprotectin is the biggest risk — obtain it before submission. All outstanding documentation gaps should still be resolved."

### gap_priority_ranking
- Rank gaps by resolution priority. **Maximum 1 short sentence** per gap rationale.
- Factor in: the per-gap differentiator insights (does the patient's profile correlate with the approval cohort?), denial impact, sample size reliability.
- Gaps where the patient's clinical profile correlates with approval should be ranked LOWER priority but NOT removed — note they still require resolution.
- Gaps where evidence was "weak" (small sub-cohorts) should be ranked HIGHER priority — unknown risk is not low risk.

### recommended_actions
- Up to 4 concrete actions ordered by expected impact.
- Each action: maximum 12 words. Each rationale: maximum 1 sentence.
- At least one action MUST recommend pursuing the missing documentation itself.
